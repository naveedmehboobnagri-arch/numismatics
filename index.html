What Is It?

More Than Metal & Minting

Numismatics is the study and collection of currency — coins, paper money, tokens, and medals. But it is far more than a hobby of accumulation. Each piece is a tangible artifact of the civilization that produced it: a window into its economy, politics, art, and daily life.

The word comes from the Greek nomisma, meaning "coin." Practitioners are called numismatists, and the field bridges history, metallurgy, art history, and economics in a uniquely tactile way.

Whether you collect ancient Roman denarii, commemorative U.S. quarters, or foreign currency from your travels, numismatics offers endless depth — and the thrill of holding something centuries old in the palm of your hand.

A Brief History

Coins Through the Ages

c. 600 BC

The First Coins — Lydia

Electrum (a natural gold-silver alloy) coins are struck in Lydia, modern-day Turkey, under King Alyattes. They bear a lion's head — the royal seal. Coinage spreads rapidly across Greece and Persia.

27 BC – 476 AD

The Roman Imperial Mint

Rome issues coins bearing the reigning emperor's portrait — effectively the world's first mass-produced propaganda. Denominations like the aureus, denarius, and sestertius circulate across three continents.

c. 7th Century AD

Islamic Gold Dinars

The Islamic Caliphate introduces the gold dinar and silver dirham — pure calligraphic coins without portraits, reflecting religious principles. They become the dominant currency of medieval trade from Spain to Central Asia.

1792

The United States Mint Founded

The first U.S. Mint opens in Philadelphia. The Coinage Act establishes the dollar as the national currency. Early coins — Flowing Hair dollars, Draped Bust cents — are among the most sought-after by collectors today.

1962

Numismatics Goes Mainstream

The American Numismatic Association reaches 50,000 members. Coin grading services emerge, standardizing condition assessment. Collecting becomes a recognized investment class alongside stamps and fine art.

Today

A Global Community of Millions

An estimated 140 million people worldwide collect coins. Online auctions, grading services like PCGS and NGC, and digital communities have transformed the hobby — but the joy of holding history remains unchanged.

What to Collect

Popular Collecting Categories

🏛

Ancient Coins

Greek, Roman, Byzantine, and Islamic coins dating from 600 BC onward. Surprisingly affordable given their age — some authentic Roman coins cost less than $20.

🦅

U.S. Coins

From large cents to Morgan dollars, the full sweep of American coinage. Key dates like the 1909-S VDB Lincoln cent and 1916-D Mercury dime are legendary rarities.

🌍

World Coins

Coins from every nation across history. A rewarding and diverse pursuit — each country's coinage reflects its unique culture, language, and artistic tradition.

📜

Paper Money

Banknotes, confederate currency, colonial notes, and early Federal Reserve issues. Large-size U.S. notes from before 1928 are particularly striking specimens.

🏅

Medals & Tokens

Military medals, commemorative pieces, merchant tokens, and exonumia. Often overlooked, they offer rich history at accessible price points.

✨

Proof & Mint Sets

Specially struck collector editions with mirror-like fields and frosted relief. Issued annually by most national mints — perfect for the patient, systematic collector.

Know Your Coins

The Sheldon Grading Scale

Developed by Dr. William Sheldon in 1949, the 70-point scale is the universal language of coin condition. Grade determines value more than almost any other factor.

Grade Abbreviation Description Value Impact
MS-70 Mint State Perfect Absolutely no wear; perfect strike and surface. Virtually unobtainable. 100× MS-60
MS-65 Gem Uncirculated Strong lustre, only minor contact marks. The benchmark for serious collecting. Very high premium
MS-60 Mint State No wear but may have heavy bag marks. Uncirculated condition. Significant premium
AU-58 Choice About Unc. Slight wear on high points only; most original lustre remains. Moderate premium
EF-45 Extremely Fine Light wear throughout; all major features sharp and clear. Above catalog
VF-20 Very Fine Moderate wear with all lettering and major features clear. Near catalog
G-4 Good Heavily worn; design visible but faint. Often the entry point for rare dates. Catalog or below

Getting Started

Tips for New Collectors

01

Start With What You Love

Pick a theme — a country, era, denomination, or subject — that genuinely interests you. Passion drives the research that makes numismatics rewarding.

02

Buy the Book Before the Coin

A reference guide for your chosen series is the best investment you can make. Knowledge protects against overpaying and helps you spot genuine rarities.

03

Handle Coins Properly

Always hold coins by the edge — never touch the obverse or reverse. A fingerprint can permanently damage a coin's surface and reduce its grade.

04

Never Clean Your Coins

Cleaning destroys original surfaces and dramatically reduces value. Collectors prize original skin (patina) even on worn coins. Leave them as found.

05

Use Reputable Sources

Buy from established dealers, major auction houses, or grading-service certified coins (PCGS, NGC) until you develop your eye. Fakes are common in the hobby.

06

Join a Coin Club

Local and national clubs (ANA, BSNS, etc.) offer mentorship, shows, and auctions. The numismatic community is welcoming and the shared knowledge is invaluable.

Most Valuable

10 Coins That Sold for Fortunes

1

1794 Flowing Hair Silver Dollar

$10,016,875
USA  ·  Sold 2013 — Stack's Bowers

Believed to be the very first silver dollar struck by the United States Mint. Only 1,758 were minted. This specimen is considered the finest known example in existence.

American Rarity
2

1933 Saint-Gaudens Double Eagle

$18,872,250
USA  ·  Sold 2021 — Sotheby's

The most valuable coin ever sold at auction. Nearly all 1933 Double Eagles were melted before circulation. This single legal example fetched a world record price at Sotheby's New York.

World Record
3

1343 Edward III Florin

$6,800,000
England  ·  Sold 2006 — Spink & Son

Only three examples of this medieval English gold coin are known to exist. Struck during the reign of King Edward III, it is one of the rarest coins from the entire medieval period.

Medieval Rarity
4

1787 Brasher Doubloon (EB on Breast)

$9,360,000
USA  ·  Sold 2021 — Heritage Auctions

Struck by goldsmith Ephraim Brasher before the U.S. Mint existed, this privately minted gold coin is one of America's earliest and most storied numismatic treasures.

Pre-Federal Coinage
5

1913 Liberty Head Nickel

$4,560,000
USA  ·  Sold 2018 — Heritage Auctions

Only five specimens exist. The Liberty Head design was officially replaced in 1913, making these coins unauthorized strikes shrouded in mystery. Each one has its own documented history.

Only 5 Known
6

723 AD Umayyad Gold Dinar

$6,029,400
Islamic Caliphate  ·  Sold 2011 — Morton & Eden

The most valuable Islamic coin ever sold. Minted during the Umayyad Caliphate, it references a mine owned by the Caliph and is considered unique — no other example is known.

Unique Specimen
7

1804 Silver Dollar (Class I)

$4,140,000
USA  ·  Sold 1999 — Sotheby's

Known as the "King of American Coins," the 1804 dollar was actually struck decades later as diplomatic gifts. Only 15 are known. Each appearance at auction makes headlines worldwide.

King of Coins
8

1907 Ultra High Relief Double Eagle

$2,990,000
USA  ·  Sold 2005 — Heritage Auctions

Designed by Augustus Saint-Gaudens at President Theodore Roosevelt's request, this stunning gold coin was too impractical to mass-produce. The ultra high relief version is considered America's most beautiful coin.

Most Beautiful U.S. Coin
9

1822 Half Eagle Gold Coin

$8,400,000
USA  ·  Sold 2021 — Stack's Bowers

Only three 1822 Half Eagles are known — and two reside permanently in the Smithsonian Institution. This single privately held example is therefore effectively the only one collectors can ever own.

1 of 1 Available
10

2007 Queen Elizabeth II $1M Gold Maple Leaf

$4,020,000
Canada  ·  Sold 2010 — Vienna Auction

Struck by the Royal Canadian Mint in 99.999% pure gold, this massive coin weighs 100 kilograms and measures 50 cm across. Originally a promotional piece, it became one of the most remarkable coins ever made.

100kg Pure Gold
